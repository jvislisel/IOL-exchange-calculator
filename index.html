How the formula works

Cheng et al., Ophthalmology 2026: the MR biometry formula.

When an eye needs a lens exchange, the original IOL has already told you something valuable: how far the real refractive outcome landed from what a formula predicted. The MR biometry formula carries that observed error forward instead of discarding it, which lets it correct for whatever caused the original refractive surprise (biometry error, lens position, and so on) rather than repeating it.

R = ( a − b ) + c
RPredicted postoperative refraction with the new IOL (what the calculator solves for, or targets).
aMeasured spherical equivalent of the manifest refraction with the original IOL still in place.
bHolladay 1 predicted refraction for the original IOL and its power.
cHolladay 1 predicted refraction for the new IOL and its power.

The term (a − b) is the observed prediction error of the original lens. Adding it to the fresh Holladay 1 prediction for the replacement lens (c) anchors the new estimate to reality. The authors chose the Holladay 1 formula specifically because it does not require phakic anterior chamber depth or lens thickness, measurements that are unavailable in a pseudophakic eye.

Worked example from the paper

A 25.0 D CCWTT3 lens exchanged for a 25.0 D LI60AO:

a
+0.75 D  (actual pre-exchange SE)
b
+0.29 D  (Holladay 1, original IOL)
c
−0.31 D  (Holladay 1, new IOL)
R = (0.75 − 0.29) + (−0.31) = +0.15 D

This calculator runs that same computation, then inverts it: given your target refraction it solves for the new IOL power whose prediction, corrected by the observed error, lands on your target. It also tabulates the nearby available powers so you can see the trade-off at each 0.5 D step.

Limitations & scope

Read before using for planning.

  • Stable, well-positioned lens required. The method depends on an accurate manifest refraction through a stable original IOL. It is not applicable to dislocated IOLs.
  • Spherical equivalent only. Like the paper, this tool predicts spherical equivalent. It does not plan toric cylinder power or axis.
  • In-the-bag assumption. Calculations assume in-the-bag placement. Expect a myopic shift with anterior optic capture, and a milder myopic shift with sulcus placement and posterior optic capture; adjust accordingly.
  • No prior corneal refractive surgery. The formula was validated only in eyes without a history of LASIK, PRK, or RK.
  • Single-center validation. The source study was retrospective and conducted at one center. Results should be interpreted with that in mind.
  • Two ways to enter data. Compute from biometry derives the Holladay 1 predictions internally from the axial length, keratometry, and A-constants. Enter from printout takes those Holladay 1 predictions directly from your biometer's printout, so the tool performs only the formula's arithmetic, exactly matching the authors' spreadsheet.
  • Independent build. This calculator is an independent implementation of the published method. It is not affiliated with or endorsed by the authors.
How this implementation is verified. The formula's arithmetic (R = (a − b) + c) has been confirmed against the study authors' own spreadsheet tool: given identical Holladay 1 inputs the two agree exactly, and both reproduce the paper's worked example. The built-in Holladay 1 engine (used in Compute from biometry mode) is a from-first-principles vergence model with constants pinned to Holladay et al. (1988), checked by an automated test suite and cross-validated numerically against an independent Holladay 1 implementation to better than 0.01 D across the clinical range. To remove any dependence on that reimplementation, use Enter from printout mode and take the Holladay 1 values from your own biometer.
